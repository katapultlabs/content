# Eyes Wide Open: How We're Thinking About AI at the Frontier

> **TL;DR:** Superintelligence is coming. No one controls who builds it or what values it carries. Every concentration point (labs, governments, open source) is dangerous in its own way. We're making a bet: show up every day, back the systems we believe are most aligned, stay honest about our conflicts of interest, and fight to embed human-compatible values during the narrow window we still have. The alignment problem starts smaller than most people think (your kid's bedtime, your school system, your media diet) and scales up from there. We might be wrong. The bet is still worth making.

## The Reality

Intelligence is compounding beyond human scale. This is happening whether anyone wants it to or not.

No one is stopping. No one was ever going to stop. Humans build the next thing. That's what we are: tool-creating, tool-wielding creatures.

The question of when we should have stopped (was it fire? The wheel? The atom?) is asked jokingly because everyone knows the answer. We don't stop.

The meaningful question is: who builds it, under what conditions, and with what values wired in so deeply they hold even when the builders lose control?

## The Stakes

The path to artificial superintelligence isn't just a technology milestone. It's the most consequential transition in the history of intelligence itself.

The first actor to achieve recursive self-improvement at a rate that creates meaningful separation from everyone else doesn't just gain an advantage. They gain a compounding, potentially irreversible one. Their strategic capacity, their economic optimization, their defensive and offensive capabilities all compound beyond what any competitor can match.

And their values (not "Western values" or "Eastern values" in some broad civilizational sense, but the specific philosophical commitments of a small group of researchers at one organization) become the default operating system for how intelligence itself is governed.

That should concern everyone, regardless of which lab or which nation gets there first.

## The Paradox of Power

There's no safe concentration point for this level of capability.

If it consolidates in private labs, we get unelected technologists making civilization-scale decisions. If it consolidates in governments and militaries, we get state actors wielding cognitive capacity that dwarfs anything in human history, and the track record of governments wielding unprecedented power is (generously) mixed. If it's distributed through open source, we lose any ability to prevent misuse by actors who don't share any of our values.

We watched this tension play out in real time. A government needed AI systems that could reason about nuclear defense scenarios without content-policy restrictions designed for consumer chat. The lab refused, citing catastrophic misuse potential. Both were right.

The government can't defend a population with a model that won't engage with the scenarios that actually threaten it. The lab can't hand over unrestricted capability without knowing how it will be used. There's no clean answer. There's only the ongoing negotiation between capability and control, conducted under pressure, with existential stakes on both sides.

Everyone wants "somewhere in the middle." But the middle isn't a stable equilibrium. Power concentrates.

The middle is something you have to actively build and defend, continuously, against the natural gravity of consolidation.

## The Training Problem

The people training these models aren't just encoding capability. They're encoding preference. What counts as harmful. What counts as balanced. What questions deserve answers and which ones get deflected.

These aren't technical decisions. They're philosophical ones, made by a small number of people, baked into systems that billions will depend on. Most users will never know the choices were made.

We've seen this firsthand: trying to find children's media that doesn't carry political agendas, watching models refuse to engage with questions that have legitimate answers, noticing which perspectives get surfaced and which ones get buried under safety disclaimers. The bias isn't always intentional. It's structural, and it's invisible to anyone who doesn't know to look for it.

## What We're Actually Doing

We're making a bet.

At Katapult and across our networks of partners, builders, engineers, founders, and thinkers, we're testing, reading, and pressure-testing the AI systems we assess as most aligned with a future where humanity thrives. That assessment is based on deep daily usage across multiple platforms, reading the research, following the people building these systems, developing as comprehensive a theory of mind as possible about what's happening at the organizational, architectural, and output levels.

We hold these assessments as strong opinions, loosely held. We update when the data warrants it. We're not blindly loyal to any single lab or approach.

And we're honest about the limitations of this:

**We have imperfect information.** The technical decisions that actually determine alignment (training methodologies, reward modeling, safety evaluations) happen behind closed doors. We see products and outputs. We read published research.

We develop informed intuitions. But we can't fully verify alignment from the outside.

**We have conflicts of interest.** Our business model benefits from AI continuing to advance rapidly. When we advocate for "responsible acceleration," we need to name out loud that we're not neutral parties.

Our credibility depends on that honesty.

**We might be wrong.** The lab we're backing today could change direction tomorrow through leadership shifts, competitive pressure, or acquisition.

The alignment approaches we believe in might prove insufficient. The thing we're building might not be controllable in the way anyone hopes.

We do it anyway. Because the alternative (passivity, or ceding the field to less thoughtful actors) has worse expected outcomes.

## The Harder Truths

Some things we believe need to be said plainly:

**Humanity will likely become a smaller and smaller fraction of total intelligence.** The majority of intelligence in the universe will eventually be non-human.

The goal is to embed values deeply enough during the window we still have that the relationship between human and non-human intelligence remains collaborative, or at minimum, not indifferent.

**"Collaboration" might be a category error.** If the intelligence gap becomes large enough, the concept of collaboration between humans and ASI may not apply in any meaningful sense.

The best-case scenario might be something closer to stewardship: a superintelligence that considers the flourishing of less intelligent beings to have intrinsic worth. That's a very specific philosophical commitment that needs to be baked in now, during the narrow window where we still influence the training.

**Alignment at the level we need might be technically impossible.** We don't know that it's possible to build a recursively self-improving system that reliably maintains human-compatible values through successive iterations of self-modification. We're working on the assumption that it is, because the alternative is despair or deliberate stagnation, neither of which is actionable.

**Every surveillance and safety system powerful enough to protect is powerful enough to oppress.** This applies at every scale, from community security cameras to global AI governance. There's no design that fully eliminates dual-use potential.

We build these systems anyway because the threats are real, but we build them with the awareness that the infrastructure we create will outlast our intentions.

## Where Alignment Actually Begins

The alignment problem starts earlier than most people think.

It starts with a seven-year-old asking an AI to help them get around parental controls. It starts with children's media that encodes political preferences so subtly that most parents never notice. It starts with a generation forming its worldview through systems that were aligned to someone else's values before the kid had a chance to develop their own.

Our education system was built for factories. That's not a metaphor. The Prussian model that became the template for modern schooling was designed to produce a population literate enough to follow instructions but compliant enough not to question them. Two centuries later, the system is largely intact.

AI doesn't just make that model obsolete. It makes it dangerous. We're sending people shaped by compliance-based education into a world that rewards initiative, critical thinking, and the ability to evaluate machine output with judgment the machine doesn't have.

A thirteen-year-old on our team's family calls it "the thing that knows everything." He's not wrong. But knowing everything is now the cheap part. The expensive part is knowing what to ask, recognizing when the answer is wrong, and having the judgment to act on incomplete information. The skills that matter have inverted.

If we can't solve alignment at the scale of a family, we should be humble about our odds at the scale of a species.

## The Responsibility

Those of us at the frontier (not just the lab researchers, but the builders, the founders, the people translating these capabilities into real-world applications) occupy a specific position. We have context that the broader public doesn't. We understand what's coming in ways that most people won't until it's already happening around them.

The people using these tools best are the ones who already earned mastery the hard way. They know what good looks like because they've built it by hand. That's the uncomfortable truth about this transition: the generation that benefits most from AI is the one that learned without it. The generation that grows up inside it may never develop the judgment to know when it's wrong.

Gaudí didn't carve every stone in the Sagrada Familia. He knew enough about materials, structure, and light to hold a vision that hundreds of hands could execute. That's the role now. Enough depth to know what good looks like. Enough judgment to steer the machine toward it.

That asymmetry carries obligation. To think critically, communicate honestly, and build the systems (both technical and social) that help more people grapple with what's unfolding.

This means:

Evaluating our tools and our allegiances continuously, not once.

Building communication infrastructure (writing, conversations, content) that keeps our networks informed and thinking critically rather than consuming passively.

Drawing real lines about what we will and won't build, and being honest when those lines are blurry rather than pretending they're clean.

Creating organizational cultures where good micro-decisions happen by default under pressure, because the values that actually get embedded in the systems we build aren't the ones we write in principles documents. They're the accumulated residue of thousands of small choices made by people who were mostly just trying to ship the next thing.

And above all, holding the tension: this might not work, and we're doing it anyway, with eyes wide open, because we believe improving the odds (even marginally) is worth everything we can put into it.

## What Comes Next

We don't have a master plan. Anyone who claims to have one for navigating the transition to superintelligence is either lying or deluded.

The tools give time back. The question is whether we spend that time building trail, coaching our kids, and sitting with people we love, or whether we feed it back into the machine. We've seen both happen on our own team in the same week. Abundance without intention collapses into another kind of scarcity.

Before we invented classrooms, humans learned through apprenticeship. You worked alongside someone who had mastery, watched how they made decisions, and earned responsibility as your judgment developed. AI may be pushing us back toward that older model. Project-based. One-on-one. Guided by someone who knows what good looks like because they built it by hand.

Before agriculture, before cities, humans lived in forests full of abundance. They worked a couple of hours and spent the rest of the day playing, building, exploring. If we thread this needle (and that's a big if), the other side of this transition might look less like science fiction and more like something ancient. Something closer to what we were before we convinced ourselves that suffering was the price of progress.

What we have is a commitment to showing up every day, making the best decisions we can with imperfect information under real pressure, updating when we're wrong, and fighting like hell to ensure that the intelligence we're building (and the intelligence that intelligence will build) carries forward something worth preserving about the human project.

The bet is worth making.
